# Vikram Samvat Calendar 
> Vikram Samvat (also called "Bikrami" or "Vikrami" in many regions across [[India\|India]] and [[Nepal\|Nepal]]) is the primary lunisolar calendar for Hindus along with the [[Hindu Panchānga\|Hindu Panchānga]]. 

Sometimes spelled "Vikrama Samvata" to combat [[Schwa-Deletion\|Schwa-Deletion]].
It is 56.7 years older than the [[Gregorian Calendar\|Gregorian Calendar]] used most often everywhere in the modern day after the Europeans practically forced it on all colonies during [[European Colonialism\|European Colonialism]].

### One Solar Day & Date Change
One Day in Vikram Samvat is counted from sunrise to sunrise i.e., the lunar date (tithi) at the time of sunrise does not change until next sunrise. The Lunar date is decided by the moon phases.

### Lunar Date (Tithi/तिथि)
Old sages who formulated the [[Hindu Panchānga\|Hindu Panchānga]] had used the process of analysing the time gap between a full moon day and a new moon day, then on averaging the gap they realised that if you remove both the Purnima (Full Moon day) and Amavasya (New Moon day) then you are left with 14 days. Dividing the fortnight into 14 equal increments (for future dates to be calculated using a formula) we get 14 days which are numbered in [[Sanskrit\|Sanskrit]].

### Lunar Months (Māsa/मास)
These 14 days were named as a Paksha (पक्ष). Each Month has 2 Paksha.
The Paksha from New Moon to Full Moon is called the Bright Half i.e., Shukla Paksha
The Paksha from Full Moon to New Moon is called the Dark Half i.e., Krishna Paksha

There exist two different systems to organise the months and decide which paksha comes first in the sequence: Purnimānta (पूर्णिमान्त) and Amavasyānta/Amānta (अमावस्यान्त/अमान्त).

Each month (depending upon the system used) begins with one paksha leading to the other.
In the Purnimānta (पूर्णिमान्त) system, the month ends on the Purnima/पूर्णिमा (Full Moon day) meaning the first paksha of any month is Krishna Paksha whereas Shukla Paksha comes first in the Amavasyānta (अमावस्यान्त) system.

70% of India (North, Central and Western India) follows the Purnimānta System, this is also the only native version available as a alternate calendar on iPhones if you wish to use both Vikrami and Gregorian together (as of iOS 26.4.2) and do not wish to juggle specific apps for this.

In the remaining 30% of India, such as Tamil Nadu and some far-east states of India, Amavasyānta system is more popular but it seems its popularity is declining (it is hard to acertain such things anyway so take the claim of "decline" with a grain of salt).

### Format of writing the Date
Dates are written with month paksha and tithi (Lunar Date) together.
The 4th of May 2026 could be written as Jyeshtha Krishna Tritiya 2083 (ज्येष्ठ कृष्ण तृतीया २०८३).
There is no fix format to write the year in the end, some may write the year at the start and some prefer to wirte the month and year after the paksha-tithi combo.

### Handling of Leap Years via Leap Months
Around 7 times every 19 years there is an addition of an Adhika Māsa (अधिक मास) which translates to an "extra month" that is added to an existing month with its Shukla Paksha (Bright Half) first regardless of the system used (Purnimānta or Amavasyānta does not matter). 

For Example, in the Vikram Samvat Year 2083, the leap month was added in the month of Jyeshtha since Jyeshtha corresponds very well to its Gregorian counterpart May 2026.

Jyeshtha 2083 corresponds to May 2026 almost exactly in the Purnimānta system. 
Since the 1st of May was a Purnima (Full Moon)

In Purnimānta system Krishna Paksha comes first, so the first day of Jyeshtha begins on the 2nd of May in the Gregorian Calendar for the year 2026. After the first Amavasya of Jyeshtha, the Shukla Paksha must begin, so instead of having the regular shukla paksha, Adhik-Jyeshtha gets its shukla paksha and krishna paksha first before the next shukla paksha is again for the original Jyeshtha.

In Amavasyānta System, Shukla Paksha comes first so the new month starts after the Amavasya anyway as usual but Adhik Jyeshtha comes first before Original Jyeshtha month.
