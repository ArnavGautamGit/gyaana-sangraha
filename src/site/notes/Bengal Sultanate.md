# Bengal Sultanate
> An independent [[Sultanate\|Sultanate]] centred around the region of present day Bengal (across the Indian state of West Bengal and the country of [[Bangladesh\|Bangladesh]]) which frequently announced independence from [[Delhi Sultanate\|Delhi Sultanate]] until it finally got its independence.

Bengal was always a distinct region which was far into the east from Delhi which served as the capital of [[Delhi Sultanate\|Delhi Sultanate]], due to its distinct culture and distinct language ([[Bengali\|Bengali]]) there is no tolerance for centralised control.

### Tughra Khan's failed Independence Attempt
The first rebellion was ushered by [[Tughra Khan\|Tughra Khan]] who was frustrated by the centralised rule from a far away city like Delhi & declared independence from the [[Delhi Sultanate\|Delhi Sultanate]] during the reign of [[Alauddin Masud\|Alauddin Masud]], 7th Sultān of Delhi from the [[Mamluk Dynasty (Delhi)\|Qutubi Mamluk Dynasty]].

Tughra Khan's army was invaded and defeated handsomely in the [[Battle of Katasin (1243)\|Battle of Katasin (1243)]] by the Hindu King [[Narsimhadeva I\|Narsimhadeva I]] of the [[Kalinga Ganga Dynasty\|Kalinga Gangas]] hard enough that Tughra had to run back to his masters in Delhi for support and the Kalinga army returned with all the money they had looted and let Delhi inherit a financially destroyed Bengal. Since Hindus are not degenerate there were no cases of mass-rapes and genocides in their lootings.

When the help sent by [[Delhi Sultanate\|Delhi Sultanate]] arrive, they deposed [[Tughra Khan\|Tughra Khan]] as a whole. 
Tughra's full name was Tughral Tughan Khan. You can search up his Wikipedia page.

### Bughra Khan's success
As the next governor after [[Tughra Khan\|Tughra Khan]]'s replacement, being the son of [[Ghiyas ud-Din Balban\|Ghiyas ud-Din Balban]] who was Sultān at the time, [[Nasiruddin Bughra Khan\|Nasiruddin Bughra Khan]] decided it is a great idea to declare Independence as his father would not do anything about it. It was true that he did not.

Although [[Nasiruddin Bughra Khan\|Nasiruddin Bughra Khan]] did get the offer to rule the [[Delhi Sultanate\|Delhi Sultanate]] after his father died, he chose to refuse the offer. It was here that Bughra's son [[Muizzuddin Qaiqabad\|Muizzuddin Qaiqabad]] was made the offer to rule and Qaiqabad accepted.

### Sequence of Sultāns (1287 to 1324 AD)
[[Nasiruddin Bughra Khan\|Nasiruddin Bughra Khan]] ---> 1287 AD to 1291 AD
[[Ruknuddin Kaikus\|Ruknuddin Kaikus]] ---> 1291 AD to 1300 AD
[[Shams ud-Din Firuz Shah\|Shams ud-Din Firuz Shah]] ---> 1300 AD to 1322 AD
[[Ghiyasuddin Bahadur Shah\|Ghiyasuddin Bahadur Shah]] ---> 1322 AD to 1324 AD

### Failed Revolt in 1328 AD
[[Ghiyasuddin Bahadur Shah\|Ghiyasuddin Bahadur Shah]] who had been appointed as the Governor after the Bengal Sultanate had been annexed into the [[Delhi Sultanate\|Delhi Sultanate]] had asserted independence just 4 years into his Governorship. [[Mohammad bin Tughlaq\|Mohammad bin Tughlaq]] sent his general [[Bahram Khan\|Bahram Khan]] to depose him and Bahram killed Bahadur Shah in battle.

Quelling this revolt remains as one of the few success of [[Mohammad bin Tughlaq\|Mohammad bin Tughlaq]]'s otherwise pitifully poor reign.

### Re-establishment in 1338 AD
This time as "Sonargaon Sultanate" ruled from the district of Sonargaon which in present-day has been partioned off to [[Bangladesh\|Bangladesh]]. This sultanate was ruled by a new set of Sultāns.
